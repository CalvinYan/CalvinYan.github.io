News Literacy


Journalism has always been about weighing the credibility of multiple sources and determining objectivity from subjectivity. I've been surprised, however, to find this subjectivity even in the world of science.


Take this article published in the New England Journal of Medicine. It claims to have found dangerous levels of hidden formaldehyde-a carcinogen-in a sample of electronic cigarettes. It would provide an excellent source in my feature story on the health effects of vaping.
I had little reason to doubt the study: it was written by reputable scientists and accepted by a reputable, peer-reviewed publication. It also confirmed my bias, and the school administration's bias, against any form of e-cigarette use.


Then I found a letter written in response to the study, claiming that the original authors were only able to detect formaldehyde under exceedingly unrealistic conditions. They said that the voltage Jensen et. al. used would overheat the e-cig and leave a nasty aftertaste, something recreational users are keen to avoid.


After considering the arguments in defense of the orignal article, I decided they lacked robustness. In my writing I took a firm stand against the claim linking e-cigarettes and formaldehyde via the fact check column, which would later become my trademark of sorts. This graphic lead some readers to mistakenly believe I was coming out in defense of vaping, but after reading the full story they retracted their accusations, for lack of a better word.

Leadership and Team Building

As the staff's digital editor, I've strived to demonstrate what I call "creative leadership": encouraging the rest of the staff to develop innovative content in print and online format.

Part of this approach involves contributing my own ideas. As an avid consumer of professional data journalism, I often come across stories that I wish to emulate in Veritas. Instead of appreciating their novelty in private, I invite others to read through them and consider how they could bring elements from these examples into our work.


At the beginning of the school year, the team was in favor of writing an editorial addressing the excessive use of air conditioning at the school and its environmental impact. Noticing that only I didn't mind the low indoor temperature, I proposed as an alternative that we look into the science behind gender disparity in preferred workplace temperature. The idea was never implemented, but is still being considered three months later.

A digital editor must also manage the logistics of digital and multimedia content. Together with Maia, we developed a comprehensive outline of topics to discuss with three key decisions to make, all of which we accomplished in an unexpectedly tight time frame of thirty minutes.


Being a creative leader means getting results efficiently, but more importantly, it means fostering an environment where each collaborator is brimming with ideas to make our publication better. I haven't fully achieved this goal yet, but I come closer with every small but meaningful measure I take.

Entrepreneurship

As the staff member in charge of Veritas's social media presence, I have a unique opportunity to maintain the publication's public relations. But securing funding is equally important.

Take a look at this data. You'll probably notice the two big spikes in engagement over the past year. Both of those posts have one thing in common: they were shared by other Facebook users.


If there's one thing I've learned about social media management, it's that there's little point in trying to build brand recognition from nothing when you have connections to rely on. That's what social networking is all about, after all.
